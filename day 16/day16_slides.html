New Vocabulary üìö

Bandwidth

Definition: The maximum amount of data that can be transmitted over an internet connection in a given amount of time, usually measured in Mbps (megabits per second).

Real-world: Think of it like a pipe carrying water - a bigger pipe (more bandwidth) can carry more water (data) at once. High bandwidth = fast downloads, smooth streaming, and no lag in games. Low bandwidth = buffering videos, slow downloads, and laggy gameplay.

Why You Should Care:

  • Netflix in 4K - Needs ~25 Mbps bandwidth
  • Gaming online - Needs consistent 3-6 Mbps (plus low latency)
  • Video calls - Needs 1-4 Mbps per person
  • Multiple users - Everyone shares the total bandwidth!
Fun Fact: South Korea has the fastest average internet speeds in the world at over 200 Mbps, while the global average is around 60 Mbps. The fastest home internet available in some US cities can reach 10 Gbps (10,000 Mbps)!

New Vocabulary üìö

Cache

Definition: A temporary storage location where your computer, phone, or browser saves copies of files, images, and data to make things load faster the next time you need them.

Real-world: Like keeping your favorite snacks in your backpack instead of going to the kitchen every time you're hungry. Your browser keeps copies of website images and files so it doesn't have to download them again, making websites load way faster on your second visit!

Why "Clear Your Cache" Fixes Things:

  • Old files causing problems - Cached version might be outdated
  • Website not loading right - Forces browser to get fresh copy
  • Login issues - Clears old session data
  • Storage space - Cache can get huge over time!
Fun Fact: Your browser cache can get HUGE! Chrome users have had cache folders over 10 GB. That's thousands of website copies sitting on your computer. Clearing cache is like cleaning out your digital junk drawer!
Trade-off: Clearing cache fixes problems BUT makes websites load slower the first time you visit them again (since they have to re-download everything).

Static Websites Review üìÑ

Static Website

Definition: A website where every visitor sees the exact same content. The pages are pre-built and don't change unless you edit the code.

Think of it like: A printed brochure - everyone who picks it up sees the same thing.

üìÑ Static Sites

  • Same for everyone
  • Fast to load
  • Simple to make
  • HTML, CSS, JS only
  • No database needed

üîÑ Dynamic Sites

  • Different per user
  • Has a database
  • More complex
  • Server-side code
  • Personalized content
Slide 11 of 24

Static vs Dynamic Examples üåê

üìÑ Static Website Examples:

  • Portfolio websites - Show off your projects
  • Landing pages - One-page marketing sites
  • Restaurant menus - Just displaying information
  • Documentation sites - Like coding tutorials
  • Blogs - Can be static (using tools like Jekyll)

üîÑ Dynamic Website Examples:

  • Social media - Instagram, TikTok, Facebook
  • E-commerce - Amazon, shopping carts, user accounts
  • Streaming services - Netflix, Spotify (personalized recommendations)
  • Email - Gmail, Outlook (different for each user)
Pro Tip: Start with static sites when learning because they're simpler, faster, and free to host!
